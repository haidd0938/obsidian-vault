# Business Analysis with Limited Data

Use this skill when:
- You need to analyze a specific company but cannot access its detailed information
- Web scraping fails due to anti-bot measures (CAPTCHAs, blocks)
- Company data is private or not publicly available
- You need to provide strategic recommendations with incomplete information

## Core Principles

1. **Acknowledge limitations upfront** – Be transparent about what you don't know
2. **Shift from specific to general** – Move from company-level to industry-level analysis
3. **Apply logical frameworks** – Use established business analysis models
4. **Incorporate regional/contextual factors** – Consider geography, market maturity, regulations
5. **Make reasonable assumptions** – Document them clearly
6. **Focus on actionable insights** – Provide value despite information gaps

### 2. Identify Analysis Scope
Determine:
- Industry/sector (e.g., construction, manufacturing, tech)
- Business model (e.g., EPC, SaaS, retail)
- Geographic focus
- Key competitive dimensions

### 3. Apply Industry Analysis Frameworks

#### SWOT Analysis (Adapted for Limited Data)
- **Strengths**: Based on typical advantages in this industry/business model
- **Weaknesses**: Common challenges for companies in this sector
- **Opportunities**: Market trends, regulatory changes, technology shifts
- **Threats**: Competitive forces, economic factors, disruption risks

#### Porter's Five Forces
1. **Competitive rivalry** – Intensity in this industry
2. **Supplier power** – Based on industry supply chain characteristics
3. **Buyer power** – Customer concentration and switching costs
4. **Threat of substitutes** – Alternative solutions available
5. **Threat of new entrants** – Barriers to entry

#### Business Model Canvas (Key Components)
- Value proposition
- Customer segments
- Channels
- Revenue streams
- Key resources/activities
- Cost structure

### 4. Incorporate Regional/Sector-Specific Factors

**For Chinese companies:**
- Government policies and incentives
- Local supply chains
- Regional competitive landscape
- Cultural/business practices
- Regulatory environment

**For specific sectors (examples):**
- **Construction/EPC**: Design-build integration, project management, local relationships
- **Manufacturing**: Supply chain efficiency, quality control, automation
- **Tech/SaaS**: Innovation speed, talent acquisition, network effects

### 5. Develop Recommendations

Structure recommendations around:
- **Operational efficiency** (process improvement, cost reduction)
- **Strategic positioning** (differentiation, market focus)
- **Risk management** (diversification, contingency planning)
- **Growth initiatives** (expansion, partnerships, innovation)
- **Digital transformation** (technology adoption, data utilization)

### 6. Present with Appropriate Disclaimers

Always include:
- "Based on industry analysis rather than company-specific data"
- "Assumes typical characteristics of companies in this sector"
- "Recommendations should be validated with actual company data"
- "Limited by publicly available information"

## Pitfalls to Avoid

1. **Overgeneralization** – Don't assume all companies in an industry are identical
2. **Ignoring uniqueness** – Leave room for company-specific factors
3. **Overconfidence** – Present analysis as informed estimation, not certainty
4. **Missing context** – Consider economic cycles, regulatory changes, market trends
5. **Static analysis** – Acknowledge that competitive landscapes evolve

## Verification Steps

When company data becomes available:
1. Cross-check assumptions against actual financials/operations
2. Adjust recommendations based on company-specific strengths/weaknesses
3. Validate regional/sector assumptions with local market intelligence
4. Update analysis with actual competitive positioning data
